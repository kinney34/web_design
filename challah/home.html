CHALLAH

Challah is a special bread in Jewish cuisine eaten on ceremonial occasions like Shabbat and other holidays (except Passover, when leavened bread is forbidden). During Shabbat (the Sabbath), people often serve two loaves of challah because in the book of Exodus, God commanded the Israelites to gather twice as much manna on Fridays so that they wouldn't have to gather any on the Sabbath. Traditionally, a small piece of dough is set aside as an offering during baking.

Disclaimer: I'm not Jewish. Not even a little bit. Any and all information I have on challah or Jewish traditions is based on internet research, and I sincerely apologize for any mistakes I may have made.

The Recipe

Unlike everyday bread dough, challah dough is enriched with eggs, oil, and sugar, and some bakers add raisins, honey, sesame seeds, anise seeds, or poppy seeds. Most recipes I found used yeast to make the dough rise, but baker's yeast wasn't actually an option until about 150 years ago, so in the spirit of Anya, I decided to use a sourdough starter. Sourdough Challah



Step 1: Make the levain

*Do this the night before you plan to bake- it needs time to rise!*

  • 113g well-fed, active sourdough starter
  • 113g warm water
  • 226g bread flour

  1. Mix the starter, water, and flour together in a medium mixing bowl. You might have to knead a bit to get it all combined.
  2. Cover the bowl with a lid or plastic wrap and let it sit at room temperature overnight (8-12 hours).


Step 2: Make the dough

  • 1/4 cup warm water
  • 3 large eggs
  • 1 1/2 tsp salt
  • 1/4 cup vegetable oil
  • 3 tbsp honey OR 1/3 cup sugar
  • about 3 cups bread flour

  1. In a large mixing bowl, whisk together the water, eggs, salt, oil, and honey (or sugar) until the salt is dissolved.
  2. With your hands or a wooden spoon, mix in the bread flour all at once.
  3. Once the flour is mostly combined, dump the dough out on a lightly floured countertop.
  4. Add the levain you made last night and knead until the dough is smooth (about 10 minutes by hand). Add a tablespoon or two of water if the dough is too stiff to work with, and a tablespoon or two of flour if it's too wet.
  5. Put the dough in a clean, oiled bowl and cover with plastic wrap. Let it ferment for about 2 hours. It probably won't rise much.


Step 3: Shape the dough

Okay, so you have a lot of options here. A brief Google search dug up 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, and 12 stranded braids, round braids, rolls, twists, knots, flowers, and keys. Step 4: Proof the dough

Put your shaped dough on a baking sheet lined with parchment paper and cover it with plastic wrap. Let it rise until tripled in size. This should take about 5 hours, but it depends on the temperature and humidity in your kitchen. Watch the dough rather than the clock; it's ready when you can gently poke it and the dough springs back.





Step 5: Bake!

  1. Preheat the oven to 350°
  2. In a small bowl, beat an egg with a pinch of salt.
  3. Brush the egg wash over the dough.
  4. Bake the dough until well browned. Rotate the pan halfway through so it browns evenly and lightly cover with foil if it's getting too dark. The baking time will vary based on how you shaped the dough; I went with a 4-strand braid and it took about 40 minutes.
  5. Once the bread is out of the oven, take it off the baking sheet and let it cool COMPLETELY on a wire rack. This is important: the bread is still baking on the inside, so if you cut into it before it's cool it will be doughy and sad. I know it's hard. I believe in you.
