Timeline of Astronomy

Information from astronomytrek.com

32500+ BCE

Early people track phases of the moon with lines carved in animal bones.

Mammoth tusk from Germany supposedly depicting the constellation Orion.

10000+ BCE

Stars used for measuring time and direction. Priests study astronomy to determine divine will, and farmers use it to predict cycle of the seasons.

4900 BCE

Goseck Circle in Germany believed to be earliest sun observatory for the purpose of measuring the path of the sun during a full solar year.

Goseck Circle

2000-3000 BCE

Sumerians and Babylonians keep earliest known astronomical records and star catalogs. Constellations of Leo, Taurus, Scorpius, Gemini, Capricorn, and Sagittarius invented.

2500 BCE

Stonehenge in Wiltshire aligned to mark summer and winter solstices.

Stonehenge

2137 BCE

Chinese astronomers record earliest known solar eclipse.

According to Chinese mythology, a solar eclipse was caused by a dragon eating the sun.

1450 BCE

Egyptians start to use sundials.

Sundial found in Egypt's Valley of the Kings.

800 BCE

Indian astronomer Yajnavalkya proposes a heliocentric view of the universe featuring a spherical earth.

600-130 BCE

Greeks develop astronomy beyond religious purposes and into a theoretical structure of the universe. Notable astronomers include Pythagoras, Thales, Plato, and Aristotle. Geocentric model of the universe widely accepted.

450 BCE

Greek philosopher Anaxagoras exiled from Athens for suggesting stars are suns similar to our own.

280 BCE

Greek astronomer Aristrachus suggests heliocentric theory of the universe. It does not catch on.

150 BCE

Antikythera mechanism, an analog astronomical computer, built in ancient Greece. Capable of predicting star and planet positions, as well as lunar and solar eclipses.

150 AD

Ptolemy refines geocentric theory of the universe in "Almagest," which listed constellations and charted the motions of stars and planets.

Ptolemy's "Almagest"

1543 AD

Copernicus publishes "De Revolutionibus Orbum Coelestium" (On the Revolutions of the Heavenly Spheres), reviving Aristrachus' heliocentric view of the universe.

Copernicus

1576 AD

Tycho Brahe compiles observations of planetary motion as evidence for Copernican system

Engraving of Brahe's observatory.
